\begin{abstract}
    Gamma spectroscopy is a widely used technique to characterize both the composition and activity of radioactive material.
    In this laboratory, we aimed to characterize the (energy-dependent) detection efficiency of a High Purity Germanium (HPGe) detector, and thus estimate the activity of a \EuSrc source of unknown age.
    We measured gamma spectra of \CsSrc, \MnSrc, \CoSrcA, \CoSrcB, and \BaSrc sources with known initial activity and age, of the \EuSrc source with known initial activity but unknown age, and of background radiation, and identified the count rates of key photopeaks at 1332, 1173, 835, 662, 356, 302, 276, 136, 133, and 81~keV, comparing using known activity and branching factors to build an intrinsic efficiency curve of the HPGe detector.
    Using this curve, we estimated its efficiency at \EuSrc peaks of 244, 344, and 779~keV to compare with branching factors to estimate the current activity of the source, and its probably age.
    We found an efficiency curve matching initial expectations, having a hump of high efficiency at low energy with steadily decreasing efficiency for higher energies.
    Using this curve, we estimated the age of the \EuSrc source to be $26.499 \pm 0.286$~years, with a manufacturing date around September 1999.
    The results demonstrate the consistency of the model of efficiency as a combination of intrinsic and geometric factors, and demonstrate (across consistent geometric layout) the effectiveness of gamma spectroscopy as a tool to measure the activity and age of radioisotope samples.
    They may be of interest to those aiming to use spectroscopy to date objects using radioactive material, and in particular to those seeking to use HPGe scintillators for this purpose.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Background}

Gamma spectroscopy is a crucial technique for characterizing radioactive material.
While the unique spectra created as a result of rich interaction phenomena from the different combinations of radiation released by radioisotopes serve well to distinguish and identify such isotopes, the intensity (count rates) of known features are also numerically useful.
Most notably, since the rate of gamma emissions at a specific energy can be predicted from isotope properties and a source's overall activity, the number of counts a detector registers near that energy can be used to estimate a \emph{detection efficiency} of the spectrometry system at that energy, and thus predict the activity of an identified radioisotope from the spectrum that same detector produces in response to it.
This efficiency is energy dependent, and the relationship may be quantified for a given detector and geometry.\\

In this laboratory, we perform gamma spectrum measurements of several sources of known composition and activity using a High Purity Germanium (HPGe) scintillator, to characterize the energy-dependent efficiency of this detector and predict the age of a \EuSrc source with known initial activity.

\subsection{Detection Efficiency}
The overall, or ``absolute'' efficiency $\varepsilon_{abs}$ of a detector, that is the ratio of decay instances detected $N$ to the known true count $S$, may be broken up into an ``intrinsic'' efficiency to the detector $\varepsilon_{intr}$, and a ``geometric'' efficiency (a.k.a. fractional solid angle) $\Omega_f$ of the given experimental setup:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:abs-eff}
    \varepsilon_{abs} = \frac{N}{S} = \Omega_f \varepsilon_{intr}
\end{equation}
A solid angle $\Omega$ (units of steradians, $0\leq\Omega\leq4\pi$) describes a field of view, in this case the angular exposure of the detector to the source.
The fractional solid angle $\Omega_f=\Omega/4\pi$, describing the fraction of radiation quanta which might even reach the detector in a given setup, can be calculated for the specific geometry of that setup, and used to estimate the \emph{intrinsic} efficiency of the detector at that energy, which can be applied to other measurements at that energy.
By taking efficiency estimates over a representative range of energies, the efficiency may be estimated at other energies (such as by using a polynomial fit).

\subsection{Specific Activity}
While the overall activity of a source might be known, this must be then interpreted using decay properties to find the activity expected for a specific type of radiation, such as the 1408~keV gamma rays emitted by a \EuSrc sample.
Thus, the true specific activity $S$ for a source of known activity $A$ measured over a sufficiently short time $T$ may be described using Eq.~\ref{eq:specific-activity}:

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:specific-activity}
    S = BF \times BR \times T \times A
\end{equation}
In Eq.~\ref{eq:specific-activity}, $BR$ represents the \emph{branching ratio}, the fraction of all decays which are of the specific decay mode associated with this energy (such as the fraction of decays which are electron capture, as opposed to the competing beta).
$BF$ then represents the \emph{branching fraction}, the fraction of decays in that mode which release the desired form of radiation (such as the fraction which then emit 662~keV gamma rays specifically).
These values have been measured and tabulated across a wide variety of isotopes and decay phenomena, and may be readily used for these specific activity and efficiency estimates.
For an isotope of half-life $t_{1/2}$ (and corresponding activity coefficient $\lambda=\ln(1/2)/t_{1/2}$), the age $t$ of a sample can be used to estimate the activity $A$ from its initial activity $A_0$, or vice versa, using the exponential decay rate law\cite{tsoulfanidis_measurement_2015}:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:exp-decay}
    A=A_0e^{-\lambda t}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Solid Angle Estimation}

The solid angle ``visible'' to the detector can be calculated by integrating over the visible area of the detector from the perspective of the source location\cite{knoll_radiation_2010}:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:gen-solid-angle}
    \Omega=4\pi\Omega_f=\int_{A}^{0}\frac{\cos\alpha}{r^2}dA
\end{equation}
In the conceptual integral of Eq.~\ref{eq:gen-solid-angle}, 
For a detector of substantial volume, this must be extended over that volume (or taken over an average, representative area), and likewise this integral must be nested and evaluated over the volume of a non-point source.\\
\defplot{intr0-sangle}{A geometric approximation, of the source as a point aligned with a perfectly cylindrical detector with radius $a$ at distance $d$, to estimate the solid angle subtended by the detector.}
This is equivalent to projecting the area of the detector onto a normalized sphere.
In the case of a sufficiently small source, aligned to the axis of a circular detector (as shown in \figref{intr0-sangle}), the circle already overlaps with a portion of a sphere with radius $R=\sqrt{a^2+d^2}$, and so this integral can be evaluated by comparing that region to the overall sphere radius:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:point-circle-sang}
    \begin{gathered}
        \Omega_f=\frac{\Omega}{4\pi}=\frac{A}{4\pi R^2}=\frac{1}{4\pi R^2}\int_{0}^{2\pi}d\phi\int_{0}^{\arctan(r/d)}R^2\sin\theta d\theta\\
        \Omega_f = \frac{2\pi R^2}{4\pi R^2}\left.\left[-\cos\theta\right]\right|_{\theta=0}^{\arctan(r/d)}\\
        \Omega_f = \frac{1}{2}\left(1-\cos\left[\arctan(r/d)\right]\right)\\
        \Omega_f = \frac{1}{2}\left(1-\frac{d}{\sqrt{r^2+d^2}}\right)
    \end{gathered}
\end{equation}
To extend Eq.~\ref{eq:point-circle-sang} to a volumetric, cylindrical detector rather than a perfectly flat circle, an average distance $\langle d\rangle$ to circular cross-sections of the cylinder (of radius $r$, height $h$, and a face distance $c$ away from the source) may be substituted\cite{npre_lab_7_manual_2025}:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:avg-dist}
    \langle d\rangle = \frac{r^2}{2} + \frac{h^2}{12} + c^2
\end{equation}
Given known dimensions, and an error in distance $\sigma_d$, the error of this estimate can also be determined:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:gen-err-prop}
    \sigma(f(x,y,\dots))=\sqrt{(\frac{df}{dx} \sigma_x)^2 + (\frac{df}{dy} \sigma_y)^2 \dots}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:sangle-error}
    \begin{gathered}
        \sigma(f(x,y,\dots))=\sqrt{(\frac{df}{dx} \sigma_x)^2 + (\frac{df}{dy} \sigma_y)^2 \dots} \\
        \sigma(\Omega_f(d))=\sqrt{(\frac{d\Omega_f}{dd} \sigma_d)^2}\\
        \sigma(\Omega_f(d))=\Omega_f\left(\frac{2\sigma_d}{d}\right)
    \end{gathered}
\end{equation}
\newcommand{\avd}{\langle d\rangle}
where, in Eq.~\ref{eq:sangle-error}, if $d$ is replaced with an average distance $\avd$, the error is likewise replaced with $\sigma_{\avd}=\frac{d\avd}{dc}\sigma_c$ for a distance to front $c$, as in Eq.~\ref{eq:avg-dist}.\\

As many detectors are of cylindrical shape\cite{knoll_radiation_2010}, these approximations can be highly useful in radiation detection, provided proper care is taken to align the source on the axis of the cylinder, and a sufficiently small source (or one at sufficiently large distance) is used.

\subsection{High Purity Germanium Scintillators}
High Purity Germanium (HPGe) scintillators are a class of semiconductor detectors with high energy resolution compared to other similar scintillators, and are thus extremely useful in spectrometry measurements such as of \BaSrc, which release gamma rays of several closely-packed energies.
Tsoulfanidis and Landseberger provide a more detailed explanation of HPGe detectors and their usage\cite{tsoulfanidis_measurement_2015}, but for the purposes of this laboratory, it is most crucial that HPGe detectors, in addition to having high energy resolution, may be stored at room temperature, requiring active cooling only during usage for best performance.

\section{Experimental Procedure}\label{sec:methods}
\FloatBarrier
We arranged an HPGe scintillator, cooling device, and multichannel analyzer (MCA)  to measure activity from a coaxially located radiation source, as shown in \figref{Lab7_setup}.
The MCA was connected to a workstation computer, and was set using the computer to provide a bias of $+3400$~V.
Crucially, the cooling device was active during all measurements to cool the HPGe detector to usable temperatures.
Dimensions of the HPGe detector, alongside other material details, may be found in Appendix~\ref{app:equip}.
We placed a marked foam block against the detector, such that the source would be placed $45\pm1$~mm from the detector.\\

We measured six sources of known composition (\CsSrc, \MnSrc, \CoSrcA, \CoSrcB, \BaSrc, and \EuSrc) and activity, alongside standalone background radiation, in order to estimate the age of the \EuSrc using the other five sources of known age.
For each measurement, we placed the source (if any) in the foam slot $45\pm1$~mm away from the detector (that is, $50\pm1$~mm from the internal crystal), corresponding to a geometric efficiency (or fractional solid angle) of $0.04492\pm0.00134$ using Eqs.~\ref{eq:point-circle-sang} and \ref{eq:sangle-error} (adjusting both solid angle and error calculations for the length of the detector).\\

\defplotW{Lab7_setup}{The HPGe detector setup used in this laboratory.}{0.8}

Each spectrum measurement consisted of placing the source (if applicable) in the foam fixture at the fixed distance aligned to the detector, after which we used the computer to initiate a spectrum recording over a set time (10 minutes each for the five fully known sources, 5 minutes for the \EuSrc source, and 20 minutes for background with no source), before swapping out for the next source, taking care not to disturb the foam mount.\\

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}
As the detector used in this experiment was of non-negligible length, an average distance was used as shown in Eq.~\ref{eq:avg-dist}.
As such, the error in the measurement of distance $c=50\pm1$~mm to the tip of the detector had to be propagated twice, first through the average distance (Eq.~\ref{eq:avg-dist}), then through the solid angle calculation of Eq.~\ref{eq:point-circle-sang}:
\begin{gather}
    \label{eq:sang-err-rederiv}
    % \begin{gathered}
    % \end{gathered}\\
    \begin{aligned}
        \sigma(f(x,y,\dots))&=\sqrt{(\frac{df}{dx} \sigma_x)^2 + (\frac{df}{dy} \sigma_y)^2 \dots}\\
        \sigma(f(x))&=\left|\frac{df}{dx} \sigma_x\right|\\
        \sigma(\Omega_f(c))&=\left|\frac{d\Omega_f}{dc}\sigma_c\right|\\
        &=\left|\frac{d\Omega_f}{d\avd}\frac{d\avd}{dc}\sigma_c\right|\\
        &\color{gray}\rightarrow\frac{d\Omega_f}{d\avd}=-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{a^{2}}{\left(\avd^{2}+a^{2}\right)^{1.5}}\right)\\
        &\color{gray}\rightarrow\frac{d\avd}{dc}=\frac{c}{\sqrt{c^{2}+\frac{a^{2}}{2}+\frac{h^{2}}{12}}}\\
        &=\left|-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{a^{2}}{\left(\avd^{2}+a^{2}\right)^{1.5}}\right)\frac{c}{\sqrt{c^{2}+\frac{a^{2}}{2}+\frac{h^{2}}{12}}}\sigma_c\right|\\
        \sigma(\Omega_f(c))&=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{a^{2}}{\left(\sqrt{\frac{3a^{2}}{2}+\frac{h^{2}}{12}+c^{2}}\right)^{3}}\right)\frac{c}{\sqrt{c^{2}+\frac{a^{2}}{2}+\frac{h^{2}}{12}}}\sigma_c
    \end{aligned}
\end{gather}

We find that the logarithmic fit of intrinsic efficiency to energy (\figref{E_v_ieff}) fairly closely matched that expected from previous experiments\cite{npre_lab_7_manual_2025}, having a region of high efficiency near low energies before gradually becoming lower and lower for higher energies.
Additionally, as seen most clearly in \figref{E_v_ieff_zoom}, the fit captures the measured efficiency at each peak within the margin of error for that peak's efficiency.
However, this requires the elimination of the peaks from \CoSrcB and \MnSrc : as seen in Table~\ref{tab:ieffs}, and Figs.~\ref{peak_835} and \ref{peak_136}, these isotopes possess such short half-lives that the samples' activity could not be feasibly separated even from the noise of the spectrum (as the background activity was already subtracted using the 20~minutes background recording), yielding results without meaning (such as a $>1$ efficiency estimate using the \CoSrcB sample).
Were we to include these uninterpretable ``data'', an unusable efficiency fit, seen in \figref{E_v_ieff_cursed} is found, yielding a much less useful age estimate of $30.67217 \pm 8.80292$~years.\\

Using the appropriate intrinsic efficiency fit, the estimates for activity of the \EuSrc source stayed relatively consistent across the three peaks (Table~\ref{tab:eu-act}), which allow us using the known original activity of the source (10~\micCi), to estimate that the sample is $26.499 \pm 0.286$~years old, created around September of 1999.\\

While we carry out error analysis accounting for errors such as in peak fitting or measurement of detector distance, we recognize that other sources of error may have affected the laboratory.
These include potential epistemic errors, such as falsely observing the \CoSrcB and \MnSrc activities to be negligible, or the polynomial fit being inappropriate (or of too high degree).
Additionally, even after subtracting background radiation, some amount of background noise can still be seen, possibly owing to time variation in background radiation, or simply random statistical phenomena.
For example, a 2204~keV peak, still visible in some spectra after background subtraction, is a typical indicator of $^{222}$Rn decay (through the daughter product $^{214}$Bi)\cite{phillips_primer_nodate}.

\section{Conclusions}\label{sec:conc}

The objectives of this laboratory, which aimed to build an efficiency curve of an HPGe detector, and use this curve to estimate the unknown activity and age of a radioactive sample, were largely met.\\

Spectra were measured of \CsSrc, \MnSrc, \CoSrcA, \CoSrcB, and \BaSrc sources for 10~minutes, of an \EuSrc source for 5~minutes, and of background radiation for 20~minutes.\\

A logarithmic polynomial fit was created of intrinsic efficiency $\varepsilon_{intr}$ as a function of energy $E$ for the HPGe scintillator using the sources of known age (all except \EuSrc), matching expected behavior with highest efficiency at a range of lower energies, and decreasing efficiency over higher energies.\\

This curve was used to estimate the current activity of the \EuSrc at $2.56954 \pm 0.03766$~\micCi, which, compared to the known original activity of 10.0~\micCi, yielded an estimated manufacturing date of September 1999.
